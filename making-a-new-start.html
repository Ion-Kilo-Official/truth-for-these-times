How to Use This Study Guide

This guide is designed to help you delve deeper into the themes presented in the sermon "Making a New Start." As you go through each section:

  • Read the summary points and key scriptures.
  • Reflect on the questions provided. Consider writing down your thoughts or discussing them with others.
  • Note the "Key Takeaways" which highlight crucial biblical principles.
  • Pray for understanding and wisdom as you study God's Word.

Introduction: The Longing for a Fresh Beginning

Have you ever wished you could take back something you said or did? Do you long for a way to erase past mistakes and start over? This study explores the biblical concept of "Making a New Start" with God, focusing on the transformative power of His grace and the significance of baptism.

The sermon uses powerful stories, like that of a notorious Moscow criminal and the Apostle Paul, to illustrate how God offers forgiveness, deliverance, and a completely new life to anyone willing to accept it.

1. God's Power to Transform Lives

The sermon highlights dramatic transformations possible through faith in Jesus. From a hardened criminal in Moscow finding peace and joining a church choir, to Saul the persecutor becoming Paul the apostle, God specializes in radical life changes.

God's love reaches us where we are, but it doesn't leave us there. He offers forgiveness and a path to a new identity in Him.

Key Scriptures:

  • 1 John 1:9:
    "If we confess our sins, He is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness."
  • Acts 22:16 (Ananias' words to Saul):
    "And now why are you waiting? Arise and be baptized, and wash away your sins, calling on the name of the Lord."

Reflection:

  • Think about the stories of the Moscow criminal and Saul/Paul. What do these examples teach you about the extent of God's forgiveness and power to change?
  • Where in your own life do you desire God's transforming power for a new start?

2. The Origin and Example of Baptism

Christian baptism finds its roots in the ministry of John the Baptist, who preached repentance and baptized crowds in the Jordan River. Jesus Himself, though sinless, came to be baptized by John.

Jesus insisted on being baptized "to fulfill all righteousness," setting a perfect example for His followers. His baptism marked the beginning of His public ministry, accompanied by the descent of the Holy Spirit and the Father's affirmation.

  • Acts 10:38 (Referenced by Peter):

Reflection:

  • Why is it significant that Jesus, who had no sin to confess, chose to be baptized? What does His example teach us?
  • What happened immediately after Jesus' baptism? What does this suggest about the connection between baptism and the Holy Spirit?

3. Baptism: A Command for All Believers

Baptism wasn't just for Jesus or the early crowds; it became a standard practice for His disciples. Before ascending to heaven, Jesus gave the Great Commission, explicitly instructing His followers to make disciples and baptize them.

The New Testament church faithfully followed this command, with thousands being baptized as they came to faith in Christ.

Key Scriptures:

  • John 4:1-2:
    "Therefore, when the Lord knew that the Pharisees had heard that Jesus made and baptized more disciples than John (though Jesus Himself did not baptize, but His disciples)..."
  • Matthew 28:19-20:
    "Go therefore and make disciples of all the nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all things that I have commanded you; and lo, I am with you always, even to the end of the age.‚Äù Amen.
  • Acts 2:41:
    "Then those who gladly received his word were baptized; and that day about three thousand souls were added to them."
Key Takeaway:Baptism is not merely an optional ritual but a direct command from Jesus for all who become His disciples.

4. How Should Baptism Be Performed? The Mode of Immersion

The sermon strongly emphasizes that the biblical mode of baptism practiced by Jesus, John, and the early church was immersion (being dipped under the water). The detailed account of Philip baptizing the Ethiopian eunuch clearly depicts them going "down into the water" and coming "up out of the water."

Historical evidence, including ancient baptistries, supports immersion as the practice for centuries. The Greek word "baptizo" itself means "to dip under." Alternative methods like sprinkling or pouring (infusion) arose later, often cited for convenience rather than biblical precedent.

Key Scriptures:

  • Ephesians 4:5:
    "one Lord, one faith, one baptism;"
  • Acts 8:36-39:

Key Takeaway:The Bible consistently points to immersion as the method of baptism, rich with symbolism representing Christ's death, burial, and resurrection.

Reflection:

  • According to the sermon, why did alternative modes of baptism arise? How does this contrast with the biblical examples?
  • Why is the symbolism of immersion (death, burial, resurrection) considered significant?

5. Born Again: The Spiritual Meaning and Necessity of Baptism

Baptism by immersion powerfully symbolizes the core of the Christian experience: dying to the old self and rising to new life in Christ. We are "buried with Him through baptism into death" and raised to "walk in newness of life."

Jesus emphasized the necessity of this experience when talking to Nicodemus, stating that one must be "born of water and the Spirit" to enter God's kingdom. He also linked belief and baptism directly to salvation.

Key Scriptures:

  • Romans 6:3-4:
    "Or do you not know that as many of us as were baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into His death? Therefore we were buried with Him through baptism into death, that just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, even so we also should walk in newness of life."
  • John 3:3, 5:
    "Jesus answered and said to him, ‚ÄúMost assuredly, I say to you, unless one is born again, he cannot see the kingdom of God.‚Äù ...Jesus answered, ‚ÄúMost assuredly, I say to you, unless one is born of water and the Spirit, he cannot enter the kingdom of God."
  • Mark 16:16:
    "He who believes and is baptized will be saved; but he who does not believe will be condemned."

Reflection:

  • How does the imagery of burial and resurrection in baptism connect with your understanding of becoming a Christian?
  • Based on Jesus' words to Nicodemus and in Mark 16, how important do you consider baptism to be in the life of a believer?

6. Preparing Your Heart for Baptism

Baptism is not just a ritual; it's an outward expression of an inward commitment. The sermon outlines key prerequisites:

  • Belief: Accepting Jesus Christ as your personal Savior and Lord, believing He died for your sins (as Philip required of the Ethiopian).
  • Understanding & Willingness: Being taught Jesus' commands and committing to follow His teachings (making disciples involves teaching).
  • Repentance & Confession: Turning away from the old life, confessing sins, and demonstrating a new direction, prompted by God's love.

True baptism follows a genuine heart change and a commitment to live for Christ.

  • Acts 8:37 (Implied - KJV/NKJV):
    Then Philip said, ‚ÄúIf you believe with all your heart, you may.‚Äù And he answered and said, ‚ÄúI believe that Jesus Christ is the Son of God.‚Äù
  • Matthew 28:20 (Part of the Commission):
    "...teaching them to observe all things that I have commanded you..."
  • Acts 2:38:
    Then Peter said to them, ‚ÄúRepent, and let every one of you be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ for the remission of sins..."

Reflection:

  • Why is commitment described as essential for baptism? How does this guard against simply "going through the motions"?
  • Reflect on the three prerequisites (belief, understanding/willingness, repentance). How do these prepare someone for the "new start" baptism represents?

7. Joining the Family: Baptism and Church Fellowship

The sermon clarifies that baptism isn't just an individual act of faith; it's also the way one becomes part of Christ's body, the Church. Those baptized on Pentecost were "added to" the fellowship of believers and continued steadfastly together.

Paul affirms that "by one Spirit we were all baptized into one body." When people commit their lives to Jesus, they naturally desire to worship and grow with fellow believers. The story of the Philippian jailer shows him and his household being baptized immediately after believing, integrating them into the community of faith.

Reflection:

  • How does the concept of being "baptized into one body" challenge the idea of solitary faith without church involvement?
  • Why is fellowship with other believers important after making a commitment to Christ through baptism?

Conclusion: Your Invitation to a New Start

God offers everyone the chance for a completely new beginning. Through faith in Jesus Christ, demonstrated publicly through biblical baptism, past sins can be washed away, and a new life, empowered by the Holy Spirit, can commence.

Just as Ananias urged Saul, the invitation stands today: If you believe in Jesus, understand His call, and are ready to turn from your past and commit your life to Him, don't wait.

Final Invitation (Echoing Acts 22:16):

"And now, why are you waiting? Arise and be baptized, and wash away your sins, calling on the name of the Lord." Will you accept God's offer of a new start today?
